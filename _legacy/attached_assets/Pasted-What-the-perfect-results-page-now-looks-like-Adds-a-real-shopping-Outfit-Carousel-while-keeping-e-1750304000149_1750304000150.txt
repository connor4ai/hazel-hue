How the outfit tiles stay “on-palette”

Color-filtered product calls
The server pings a fashion-search API (e.g., ShopStyle Collective, Amazon PA-API, or a visual-search service like Ximilar) with a color or HEX filter that comes straight from the user’s palette.
• ShopStyle supports ftsColor or minPrice/maxPrice in its /products endpoint. 
pypi.org

• Amazon’s PA-API SearchItems call lets you pass Color as a variation dimension.
webservices.amazon.com

• Services such as Ximilar or Lykdat add AI tagging if you need more granular hue matching.
ximilar.com
Outfit assembly logic
Pick 2–4 complementary colors from the palette (e.g., one neutral + one wow) → pull tops, bottoms, layers, and accessories that match those colors → bundle them as a “look.”
Behind the scenes you store each look as an array of product URLs; the front-end just renders the hero image and forwards the user if they click.
Affiliate-ready
Because links come from ShopStyle/Amazon/etc., every “Shop this look” click can already include your affiliate tag—no extra work later.
Experience details that delight shoppers

Live price labels – Show $55-$80 pulled from the API, so the user knows it’s real stock, not inspiration.
Hover / tap color check – On desktop, hovering any outfit tile outlines the parts that match the user’s palette; on mobile, a quick tap toggles the highlight overlay.
Out-of-stock fallback – If a product 404s, auto-replace it with the nearest-color alternative before rendering.
Privacy & cleanup – PDFs and uploaded selfies auto-purge after 24 h; product links stay live because they’re hosted by the retailer.
Net result for the end user
They learn what colors suit them (Cards 1–3).
They can instantly buy clothes that already match those colors (Card 4).
They can print, share, or revisit whenever they like (buttons + share link).
